1. Start Here

Foundations Before the Library Swallows You Whole

Start with a few anchor texts, then move outward. This page uses short introductions adapted from Wikipedia to keep the first step clear, while sending the full reading outward to known archives and reference pages.

The Communist Manifesto

First published in 1848, Marx and Engels' pamphlet frames class struggle as the engine of history and calls on workers to organize as a political force.

Read on Marxists.org Wikipedia overview

Das Kapital

Marx's major critique of political economy studies commodities, value, exploitation, and accumulation to explain how capitalist production reproduces its own social order.

Read on Marxists.org Wikipedia overview

Socialism: Utopian and Scientific

Engels contrasts earlier socialist visions with historical materialism and lays out why socialism, in this tradition, is treated as a development emerging from real social conditions.

Read on Marxists.org Wikipedia overview

Critique of the Gotha Programme

This 1875 text is Marx's sharp response to a German party draft programme and remains central to debates about transition, distribution, and the meaning of communism.

Read on Marxists.org Wikipedia overview

The State and Revolution

Written in 1917, Lenin's book argues that the state is bound to class power and insists that revolution means transforming, not simply inheriting, the old state machinery.

Read on Marxists.org Wikipedia overview

What Is to Be Done?

Lenin's pamphlet argues for a disciplined revolutionary organization and is often read as a key text on party-building, strategy, and political education.

2. Essay

A Small Door Into a Very Large Argument

Communism is not one book, one party, one state, or one century. It is a field of arguments about exploitation, ownership, class power, crisis, planning, solidarity, and what a life beyond wage dependence could mean.

That means the beginner's task is not to memorize slogans. It is to learn how the tradition explains capitalism, how different currents disagree about strategy, and how theory and history keep revising one another.

This page is designed as a first room rather than a final answer: read a short foundational text, move to theory, check the history, and come back to practice with better questions than you started with.

3. Theory

Core Ideas to Track While You Read

Class

Classes are defined by their position in production, not by taste or income alone. The key question is who owns, who works, and who controls the surplus.

Historical Materialism

Social life changes through concrete material relations and conflict, not simply because ideas appear in the abstract.

Value and Exploitation

Capitalist profit depends on labor producing more value than it receives back as wages, which turns work itself into a site of domination.

State Power

The state is not neutral in this tradition. It is treated as part of the structure through which class rule is organized and defended.

Internationalism

Capital crosses borders, so resistance and solidarity cannot be limited to one nation without leaving the system intact.

Transition

A recurring dispute asks what comes between capitalism and communism: party, council, commune, state, federation, or some shifting combination of them.

4. History

Moments Worth Returning To

1848

Manifestos and Revolutions

The Communist Manifesto appears during a wider season of European upheaval, binding theory to agitation from the beginning.

1871

The Paris Commune

The Commune becomes a lasting reference point for debates over worker power, democratic control, and the fate of the state.

1917

Russian Revolution

A revolutionary break becomes state power, opening both a new political horizon and a century of arguments over strategy, authority, and bureaucracy.

1949-1979

Global Revolutions and Anti-Colonial Struggle

Communist movements become entangled with decolonization, peasant war, national liberation, and competing models of socialist development.

Now

Afterlives and Re-readings

Current study often returns to old texts through the lenses of logistics, care, ecology, racial capitalism, and platform labor.

5. Practice

What to Do With the Reading

Build a Reading Ladder

Begin with a pamphlet, then pair it with one historical case and one disagreement from inside the tradition.

Keep a Terms Notebook

Track recurring words such as labor, value, ideology, state, and reproduction. Definitions shift depending on the author.

Read Collectively

A difficult text becomes usable much faster when read in a small group that can compare interpretations and examples.

Test Theory Against Life

Relate each concept back to housing, work, debt, logistics, schooling, healthcare, and the institutions shaping daily life.
